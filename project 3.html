Universities Are Failing Student Mental Health

The Crisis Goes Beyond Counseling Waitlists

A Growing Crisis

Mental health problems are the most burdensome disease among adolescents in the United States. Students suffer from stress, depression, anxiety, and eating disorders, with three-quarters of lifetime mental disorders appearing between ages 18 to 24.

These problems lead to high dropout rates, lower academic performance, and lifelong consequences for employment and income. Universities have both an opportunity and responsibility to address this crisis during these critical years.

The Resource Shortage

While student demand for mental health services has doubled, university resources have expanded slowly. The number of students seeking help rose 35% since 2014, while counselor numbers changed little.

1:4,000

At Utah Valley University, one counselor serves 4,000 students, far exceeding the recommended 1:1,500 ratio.

Real Student Experiences: A student at Utah Valley University with anxiety was told wait times could be months long and left without help. A suicide at Brigham Young University prompted complaints about therapy wait times.

The Psychological Wall

A World Health Organization study found only one in four students would seek help for serious mental health issues. Most who don't seek help are afraid to ask for support.

1 in 4

Only one in four students with serious mental health issues would seek professional help.

"The humiliation of being judged is one of the biggest reasons why students will not ask for help."

Students feel embarrassed, nervous, and fear being seen as weak. Some worry seeking help might affect their academic reputation.

Effective University Responses

Some universities show how to address both resource shortages and psychological barriers.

Kent State University's Approach

Increased counseling staff by 50% over 4 years, ensured staff diversity reflected students, and implemented online screening tools. Result: Psychological appointments increased 9%, individuals served rose 4%.

Learn about JED Foundation programs →

University of Aberdeen's Model

Created mental health governance groups, enhanced online resources, and promoted wellbeing activities like 'Walk This May'. Result: High engagement and increased interaction with mental health services.

Research shows technology-based interventions help overcome barriers. Online therapy can reduce embarrassment and reach students who stay quiet about their struggles.

A Call for Smarter Solutions

Universities must address both resource shortages and psychological barriers. Research shows depression significantly predicts lower GPA and higher dropout rates.

This isn't just about hiring more staff—though that's needed. It's about implementing smarter approaches that destigmatize mental health support and integrate it into campus life.

The examples of Kent State and Aberdeen show what's possible with comprehensive approaches. All universities should adopt similar practical actions to support student mental health effectively.
