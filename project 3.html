Universities Are Failing Student Mental Health

The Crisis Goes Beyond Counseling Waitlists

A Growing Crisis

Mental health problems are the most burdensome disease among adolescents in the United States.

Three-quarters of lifetime mental disorders appear between ages 18 to 24. These problems lead to high dropout rates and lower academic performance.

The Resource Shortage

Student demand for mental health services has doubled, but university resources have expanded slowly.

1:4,000

One counselor serves 4,000 students at Utah Valley University, exceeding the recommended 1:1,500 ratio.

A student with anxiety was told wait times could be months and left without help.

For more on this: Colleges struggle with demand →

The Psychological Wall

A World Health Organization study found only one in four students would seek help for serious mental health issues.

1 in 4

Only one in four students with serious issues seeks professional help.

Students feel embarrassed and fear being seen as weak. Some worry help might affect their academic reputation.

These barriers are documented: Barriers to help-seeking →

Effective University Responses

Some universities show how to address both shortages and barriers.

Kent State University

Increased counseling staff by 50%, ensured staff diversity, and implemented online tools. Result: Appointments increased 9%.

JED Foundation programs →

University of Aberdeen

Created mental health groups and promoted activities like 'Walk This May'. Result: High engagement with services.

Aberdeen case study →

Online therapy can reduce embarrassment and reach students who stay quiet.

A Call for Smarter Solutions

Universities must address both shortages and barriers. Depression predicts lower GPA and higher dropout.

This isn't just about hiring more staff. It's about destigmatizing support and integrating it into campus life.

Kent State and Aberdeen show what's possible. All universities should adopt similar approaches.
